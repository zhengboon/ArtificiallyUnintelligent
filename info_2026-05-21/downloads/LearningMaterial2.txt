TVIiTsuLaEl Odometry

SUBTITLE

· Visual Odometry (VO) is the process of
    estimating the position and
    orientation (pose) by analyzing the
    changes that motion induces in
    adjacent camera images.
     · Adjacent images can be frames
         after frames or stereo camera
         images.

· Think of it like a human walking in the
    dark with a flashlight: you know how
    far you've moved by watching how
    landmarks (a chair, a rug, a
    doorframe) shift in your field of
    vision.

· Pros: Easily deployable

THoITwLiEt works (high level pipeline)

SUBTITLE

· Step 1. Image Acquisition: The camera
    captures frames at a high rate (e.g., 30
    fps).

· Step 2. Feature Detection: The software
    identifies "keypoints" in the image--sharp
    corners, edges, or unique textures.

· Step 3. Feature Tracking/Matching: The
    system looks for those same keypoints in
    the next frame.

· Step 4. Motion Estimation: By measuring
    the geometric displacement of these
    points, the system calculates the
    camera's movement (X, Y, Z and rotation).

· Step 5. Local Optimization: It constantly
    "polishes" the path to reduce small
    calculation errors.

TLiImTLitEations to Keep in Mind

 SUBTITLE

· The "Drift" Problem: Because VO calculates
    motion relative to the previous frame, tiny
    errors add up over time. Without a global map
    (SLAM) or GPS to correct it, the estimated
    position will eventually "drift" away from
    reality.

· Low-Texture Environments: VO struggles in
    rooms with plain white walls or over calm
    water, as there are no "features" to track

· Motion Blur: Rapid, jerky movements can blur
    the image, making it impossible to match
    keypoints accurately.

· Lighting: Extreme darkness or blinding glare
    can "blind" the algorithm.

dSUBiTfITfLeE rence?

· Visual Odometry: Only cares about the local
    path (where am I compared to 1 second ago?).
    It is computationally "light.

· Visual SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and
    Mapping): Builds a permanent map of the
    whole environment and recognizes when it has
    returned to a previous spot (Loop Closure) to
    fix all accumulated drift. It is much more
    "heavy" on the processor.

· As such, the Qualifier will only require teams to
    achieve some simple map without visual slam
    due to Visual SLAM's heavy processor demand.

WTIThaLtEis a Depth Camera?

SUBTITLE

· A depth camera (also known as an RGB-D
    camera) is a type of sensor that provides
    more than just a 2D color image. It
    captures spatial data, allowing the system
    to know exactly how far away every pixel
    in the frame is from the lens.

· Depth Camera provides color (R, G, B) +
    distance (D).

· The Result: A 3D "snapshot" of the
    environment rather than a flat projection.

                                                                                                                                                                                                             17
XXX

TDIeTpLtEh Camera Technologies

SUBTITLE

· Depth cameras use one of three primary
    technologies to estimate distance:
     · Stereo Vision: Uses two cameras
          slightly apart (like human eyes). By
          comparing the horizontal shift of
          objects between the two images
          (disparity), the distance is calculated.
     · Structured Light: Projects a known
          pattern of infrared dots or lines onto a
          scene. The camera observes how the
          pattern deforms on surfaces to
          calculate depth.
     · Time-of-Flight (ToF): Emits a light
          pulse and measures the nanoseconds
          it takes for the light to bounce off an
          object and return.
     · Depth Estimation can also be done
          using one camera (Monocular Depth
          Estimation)

                                                                                                                                                                                                             18
XXX

TMIoTnLoEcular Depth Estimation

SUBTITLE

· Depth Estimation can also be done using
    one camera.

· Often, a single image doesn't contain
    information at the correct scale.

· So, early monocular depth estimation
    methods relied on straightforward visual
    rules tied to camera geometry. Cues like
    perspective, object size, and whether one
    object blocked another were used to
    estimate distance.

· Most modern monocular depth estimation
    systems use deep learning, which refers
    to neural networks with many layers that
    can learn complex patterns from data.
    These models learn to predict depth
    directly from images and produce depth
    maps.

                                                                                                                                                                                                             19
XXX

HTIoTwLEmonocular depth
eSUBsTItTLiEmation models work

· Most monocular depth estimation models
    follow a similar process to convert a
    single image into a depth map:

      · Input and preprocessing: The original
           image is usually resized, normalized, and
           converted into a tensor.

      · Feature extraction: An encoder network
           analyzes the image to extract meaningful
           visual features such as textures, object
           boundaries, and the overall layout of the
           scene.

      · Depth reasoning: At this stage, it learns
           which regions of the image are closer to
           the camera and which are farther away.

      · Depth map generation: A decoder then
           converts this information into a dense
           depth map. Each pixel in the image is
           assigned a depth value, often by blending
           predictions from different scales to
           improve accuracy and consistency.

TWIhTatLdEoes float32 mean?                                                     27

S·UBTEITLaEch pixel value uses 4 byte to represent
    distance in meters from camera optical
    center to surface.

· See line 13 to 21, especially line 13 which
    tell numpy how to interpret the data.

· Range:
     · 0.0 (invalid/no return)  near_clip 
         far_clip (e.g., 10.0m)

· Precision:
     · ~1mm accuracy in typical ranges
         (sufficient for drone navigation)

· Special Values:
     · 0.0 or NaN: No valid depth (ray hit sky,
         transparent material, or out of range)
     · inf: Clipped at far boundary

· Why not uint16?
     · uint16 requires scaling (e.g., mm =
         value/1000). float32 stores meters
         directly, simplifying 3D math and
         point cloud generation.

 TSuImTmLaErizing the depth data

   SUBTITLE

  · The depth_receiver.py provides a depth_map, 2D
       array that describes distance of objects from the
       stereo camera which is quite challenging if it is not
       pre-processed or summarized.

  · First, segment depth_map into equal region based
       on num_of_bins in the code. This could
       something that you could tweak to affect the
       performance. Then the following is done on distance
       data in each region:
        · A distance value of that region is determined
             by calculating 20 percentile of each region,
             excluding NAN data.
        · Then that distance value is used to get a cost
             value where distance less than
             critical_distance gets highest cost.
        · Angle of the center of the region is calculated
             using the camera's intrinsic values (focal length
             of the camera along x axis and principal point x-
             coordinate).

 TUsIiTngLtEhe summarized data to make

 sSUpBeTIeTLdE decision
